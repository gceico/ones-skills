**Deep research: the golden rules of 1:1 LinkedIn cold/warm outreach, and how outreach platforms actually implement them**

I'm building an assisted (human-approved, one-at-a-time) LinkedIn outreach system: harvest warm leads (recent connections, accepted invites) into a pipeline, bucket them, and run a short pre-approved message sequence — max 3 touches, spaced over days, with a hard rule that a lead is dropped the moment they reply or engage. Messages are drafted by AI, edited/approved by a human, then sent manually. No mass automation.

I want to battle-test this methodology against industry standard and pressure-test my assumptions. Research and answer with specifics, numbers, and citations:

**1. Sequence structure & cadence (evidence-based)**
- Connection note → first DM → follow-ups: what's the proven structure? Should step 1 have a note or be noteless (data shows noteless invites often accept higher — verify)?
- Optimal number of touches before stopping. Is 3 right, or do reply rates justify 4–6? Where's the point of diminishing returns / annoyance?
- Optimal intervals between touches (days). What do the best-performing sequences use, and why?
- Best days/times to send, and does it actually move reply rates or is it folklore?

**2. Message anatomy that gets replies**
- Hook selection, personalization depth vs. scale (does line-level personalization beat templated at 1:1 volume?), CTA design (soft question vs. call ask), ideal length (character counts / reply-rate curves).
- Warm (post-accept) vs. cold framing — what changes.
- Concrete before/after examples of messages that convert vs. flop.

**3. Benchmarks by relationship degree**
- Connection accept rates, reply rates, meeting-booked rates for 1st vs 2nd vs 3rd degree. What's good vs. average vs. bad in 2025–2026?

**4. What kills reply rate + trips detection**
- Message patterns that read as automated / spammy and tank replies.
- LinkedIn's spam/automation detection: what triggers restrictions, weekly invite/message limits, warm-up limits, account bans. Assisted-manual vs. automated-tool risk.

**5. Stop signals**
- Which reply/engagement signals should immediately halt a sequence (reply, reaction, profile view, out-of-office). Best practice on re-engaging later.

**6. How the leading outreach platforms actually implement this**
- Compare how Waalaxy, Expandi, Dripify, HeyReach, Lemlist, La Growth Machine, Dux-Soup, Skylead handle: sequence templates, delays/randomization, reply detection & auto-stop, safety limits, personalization/{variables}, multichannel. What are their default cadences and stop rules — and what do practitioners say they get wrong?

**7. ToS & ethics**
- LinkedIn's stance on automation vs. assisted sending. Where's the line for a human-in-the-loop tool. Reputational/deliverability risks.

**8. Golden rules & common mistakes**
- The consensus "golden rules" from top practitioners (cite Josh Braun, Lavender, Morgan Ingram, Belkins, LinkedIn's own guidance, cold-outreach studies).
- The most common mistakes that waste the outreach — ranked.

**Output:** Tight, actionable rules I can drop into a methodology reference doc, with a section flagging where my assumptions (3 touches, [0/+4/+7 day] spacing, drop-on-any-engagement, minimal name-only personalization, human-approved manual send) are backed by evidence vs. where the data says I should reconsider. Cite sources throughout with dates.
